# Orbit

I've always had a complicated relationship with to-do apps. I'd download one, use it for three days, feel overwhelmed by the list I'd built, and quietly delete it. The problem was never the app — it was that a flat list of tasks feels lifeless. There's no sense of scale, no way to see which things actually matter, and no feeling of momentum when you tick something off. It just disappears and the list gets shorter for a moment before you fill it again.

The idea for Orbit came from thinking about how goals actually work in your head. You don't think "email client, buy milk, finish report" as equals. You think in gravity — some things are central, massive, pulling everything else around them. A project you've been putting off for weeks has a different weight than a quick errand. I wanted to build something that felt like that.

The orbit view was the original spark. I kept imagining a goal sitting at the center like a sun, and all the steps needed to reach it spinning around it as planets. When you complete a step it doesn't just disappear — it escapes. It spins up, breaks orbit, and flies off the screen. That one animation took me longer than almost anything else in the project, and I don't regret a single hour of it.

The rest of the app grew around that core idea. A normal task list for day-to-day things. A schedule view so you can see what's coming. Analytics because I wanted to understand my own patterns, not just be told I'm doing great. People, because some tasks belong to other people in your life and it matters to track that separately.

I called it Orbit because everything in the app revolves around something. Your tasks orbit your goals. Your goals orbit your week. Your week orbits whatever kind of life you're trying to build. It's a small metaphor, but it kept me honest about what the app was actually for.

This is a personal project. I built it because I wanted to use it myself, and because I'd been meaning to build something full-stack from scratch for a while and kept putting it off. Starting it was its own kind of orbit problem.

---

Built with React, Node.js, Prisma, and PostgreSQL. Animated with Framer Motion. The galaxy background is hand-drawn with canvas.
